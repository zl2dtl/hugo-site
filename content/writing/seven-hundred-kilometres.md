# Seven Hundred Kilometres (or there abouts)

I got back from Auckland yesterday.

Six hundred and fifty-eight kilometres away from Wellington, if we're being precise, which turned out to be just far enough for my brain to start behaving differently. Or perhaps it was the eight and a half hours spent getting there.

By the time you've seen the ninety-eighth farm on the same road, eighty-five kilometres since the last turn and another hundred and five until the next, you've had time to think, to say the least. And think I did.

The conference itself was excellent. Most of it was held in a fairly standard conference centre, which is considerably less exciting than it sounds when described that way, but the people made up for it. More importantly, I met the only two other young people there.

What I actually found were two nerds who occasionally managed to make my own level of nerdiness seem quite moderate by comparison. Usually I'm the one disappearing down obscure technical rabbit holes while everyone else slowly regrets asking a question. It was refreshing to spend a few days around people who could not only keep up but occasionally leave me behind entirely.

Yesterday morning, before heading home, we visited an amateur radio club operating from a historic World War II radio station. That was probably my favourite part of the trip. There is something fascinating about standing inside a building that exists because, decades ago, people were trying to solve exactly the same problem we're still trying to solve now.

Humans standing in one place, attempting to communicate with humans somewhere else.

Different technology. Same objective.

There were cellular antennas on the site too, and enough expensive coaxial cable hanging around to make me slightly nervous, but honestly the history interested me more. The equipment changes. The problem never really does.

The conference was busy enough to be interesting but not busy enough to be exhausting. There was always something to do, somewhere to be, someone to talk to, but nobody particularly needed anything from me. It turns out that's quite a rare state of affairs.

Most of my life is spent thinking about whatever is directly in front of me: school, projects, computers that are either broken or about to be, radios, things I should probably finish, things I should probably start, whether I can afford the next project, and whether I can get out of the next party invitation without offending anybody. The usual collection of nonsense.

But after eight and a half hours of driving and three days with very little demanding my attention, I found myself thinking about what I wanted to think about instead. Which turned out to be a rather different thing entirely.

We had a nice phone call while I was away, again nothing more than a regular catch up.

The odd thing was everything in between.

I kept noticing things and mentally setting them aside: a joke, a story, something ridiculous somebody said, a strange observation, something that happened during the day. Not because any of those things were especially important, most of them weren't, but because I already knew who I'd want to tell about them later.

And that's when I noticed something I probably should have noticed more consciously a while ago.

A surprising amount of my life consists of mentally collecting things for her.

Not because she particularly cares about amateur radio, computers, printers, conferences, or any of the other things that occupy far too much of my attention. She doesn't. At least I hope not. If I ever hear her voluntarily discussing antenna systems or trying to learn most of what I know, I'm almost certainly being left forever.

But she listens anyway. Not because the subject matters, but because I'm the one talking about it.

And the more I thought about that, the more I realised I do exactly the same thing in reverse.

The thing itself doesn't always matter. The person attached to it does.

I spent most of the trip thinking. The funny thing is that the more I thought, the less I found myself thinking about the conference at all.

Every line of thought eventually wandered back to the same place. Not because I suddenly realised anything. I've known for a long time now.

If anything, the trip simply reinforced what was already there.

Every interesting thing I see that I want to share. Every joke that only really works if she's there. Every moment where my brain has already decided who I'd like to tell before I've consciously made the decision.

I keep arriving at the same conclusion from entirely different directions.

If feelings can be quantified at all, which I strongly suspect they can't, then mine resemble a logarithmic function. Always increasing, never quite flattening out, growing more slowly than before perhaps, but still growing nonetheless.

A terrible analogy. But then again, most attempts to explain love using mathematics tend to be.

The truth is that I almost never say it directly. Not because I don't mean it, but because it feels like one of those facts that doesn't need repeating to remain true, even though at the same time it does. And honestly, I'm just not used to it.

Every now and then something gives me enough distance to think properly. A long drive, a few days away from home.

And every single time, without exception, I end up in the same place.

Not because anything has changed. Just because it was already true.

I know who my person is. I know who I want to tell things to.

And I know, with a level of certainty that is honestly quite alarming, that she is the only person I will ever love this way for the rest of my life.

If anything, the distance simply gave me enough time to put it back into the front of my mind.

With love,
Daniel
