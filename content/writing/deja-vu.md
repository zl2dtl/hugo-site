It's been a fairly boring week, which is unfortunate because those are significantly harder to write about.

School happened. Accounting happened. Physics happened. English happened. Another week quietly disappeared into the pile of weeks that came before it.

The interesting things all happened outside of school.

Last weekend disappeared almost entirely into the ThinkPad X1 Nano I'm writing this on now. What started as a laptop with no signs of life ended with me soldering wires directly onto a BIOS flash chip, reconstructing firmware images, and spending far too much time staring at hexadecimal data. By Sunday night it was working again. If you are slightly more technically inclined, you can read that one [here](https://notes.zl2dtl.xyz/thinkpad)

Monday and Tuesday were spent documenting the repair and finally setting up the website I've been meaning to make for months (the one linked above). There's something satisfying about finally having a place for these projects to live. Computers are temporary things. Hard drives fail. Laptops get dropped onto physics classroom floors. Documentation survives slightly better.

Wednesday brought a new handheld radio. Thursday was spent programming it.

And then there was the Samsung.

I've been working on an S23 Ultra for the last couple of days. One of those projects where every solution leads to a new problem and every promising avenue turns into a dead end. The sort of repair that slowly convinces you that everybody else is right and what you're trying to do probably isn't possible.

In this case I was trying to recover a phone locked behind Google's Factory Reset Protection system. Most approaches you'll find online either no longer work because Samsung patched everything past Android 14 or require access to tools that ordinary repair technicians simply don't have (or in my case, can't afford).

I'd spent hours chasing ideas that went nowhere when another one occurred to me.

The phone was mostly stock, but there were a few firmware components I could modify, and others that matched the Android 13 equivalents 'closely enough'. Nothing particularly dramatic. Just enough flexibility to try something I hadn't seen documented anywhere else.

I flashed it.

The phone rebooted.

And suddenly I had ADB access.

That was the moment I sat back slightly.

The phone was still locked.

I still didn't have a solution.

But for the first time all day, the device was doing something genuinely new.

Up until then every path I'd followed had ended exactly where everyone said it would. Now I had something different sitting in front of me.

It felt like progress.

I was on the phone with her at the time, which is not particularly unusual. The unusual part was that she'd largely stopped paying attention to what I was doing and had instead started scrolling Instagram.

There is a certain type of phone call we have where one of us is doing something and the other is just sort of present. Not really discussing anything important. Just existing in the same conversation.

She was describing reels. I was staring at firmware. Neither activity had much to do with the other.

Then somewhere in the middle of a sentence about something I have completely forgotten, I had another idea.

One of those ideas that appears fully formed without any warning.

I typed a command. The phone rebooted.

A few seconds later I realised it had actually worked.

I think my exact response was "Holy shit."

The phone got dropped onto the desk because I immediately forgot I was holding it.

Hours of dead ends, and the solution ended up being a single line typed into a terminal.

The lock was gone. Just like that.

And now, somewhat absurdly, it's my phone.

What struck me afterwards wasn't the success itself. It was the feeling.

I've felt it before.

Four years ago now, back in Year 8, I was fighting with a da Vinci 1.0A 3D printer. A truly terrible machine which I loved completely.

That project seemed determined to resist every attempt at progress. Every solution revealed a new problem. Every breakthrough was followed by another obstacle. Looking back, I learned more from that printer than almost anything else I've worked on.

And throughout all of it, she was there.

Not helping, exactly. Not in any technical sense. Just there.

The funny thing is that if you look at the actual facts, these situations have almost nothing in common.

Different projects, different schools, different versions of me. Four years apart.

Yet somehow I found myself sitting at my desk yesterday with exactly the same feeling.

Hours of frustration. An apparently impossible problem.

And her voice in the background talking about something completely unrelated.

The rational part of my brain would like to point out that correlation does not equal causation.

She's not secretly responsible for repairing printers or bypassing Samsung firmware locks. That would be absurd.

But I do find it slightly suspicious that some of my favourite project memories seem to involve her doing absolutely nothing and everything simultaneously.

Maybe it's a coincidence.

It almost certainly is.

But then again, some things matter regardless of whether they can be explained properly.

Four years ago I was sitting in Year 8 fighting with a printer.

Now I'm sitting here writing on a laptop I repaired last weekend and carrying a phone I rescued yesterday.

A lot has changed. One thing hasn't (for the most part).

That's not nothing.

With love,

Daniel
