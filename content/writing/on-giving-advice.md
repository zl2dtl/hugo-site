I promised a post today. I got swamped. This is me making good on it at an unreasonable hour, which feels appropriate given the subject matter.

People come to me for advice. I know that sounds like something you'd put on a CV and immediately regret, but it's just true. I've somehow developed a reputation for it, and I think I know why. I don't dress things up. If you come to me with a problem I'm going to tell you what I actually think, not a softened version of it designed to make you feel better in the short term. I can solve problems, or at least I'm good at trying, and I don't see much value in telling someone what they want to hear when what they need to hear is sitting right there.

For most people I don't think twice about it. Someone comes to me, I help, that's the end of it. It's almost mechanical in a way that I don't mean negatively. Problem in, solution out, move on.

And then there's the other situation. The one where someone who is arguably closer to me than anyone else is dealing with something, and I seize up completely.

It's not that I don't want to help. I want nothing more than that, genuinely, unreservedly. And I should say, nobody asked me to fix anything. That's the thing. This is entirely self-imposed. The machinery that makes me want to help people runs whether or not it's been switched on by anyone else, and sometimes it just starts up on its own and I'm left standing there holding a solution to a problem I haven't actually been given.

The harder part is that even if I were asked, I'm not sure I'd know what to do. Everyone copes differently. Everyone deals with things differently. The way I handle something and the way another person needs to handle the same thing can be completely unrecognisable from each other. I cope by writing, or in less functional periods by disappearing from the world entirely for a few months and not talking to anyone. Neither of those is a template I'd hand to someone else. And there are some things where my own experience is so foreign to the situation that I couldn't even draw on it as a reference point. I'd be solving blind, which is a feeling I find deeply uncomfortable.

So I don't know which version of myself to be. Whether to listen or to speak, whether to sit with it or try to fix it. I am genuinely, deeply bad at not knowing things. I have spent most of my life building systems and solving problems specifically to avoid that feeling. It is probably the thing I am worst at tolerating.

And then I spend so long thinking about all of this that by the time she actually asks me to think about something, a poem she wanted me to read and analyse, I had nothing left. I don't have the best relationship with poetry at the best of times, it's vague and hard and I tend to avoid it, but usually I can articulate something. Tonight I had burned through everything I had on a problem that by tomorrow will almost certainly have dissolved into nothing. The elephant in the room that turns out to be a fleck of dust. A thing I built in my head because that's what I do.

Timing is its own post, one I've written before in an earlier life when this was still pretending to be a tech blog and I was weaving circuit metaphors into writing about her. I'll redo that one soon, probably tomorrow, which at this point is technically today.

For now, I'm working on it. The not knowing. The sitting with things that don't have clean answers. The turning off the problem-solving machinery when nobody asked me to turn it on.

The desire to fix things, I've realised, comes from a place of love. It's just very misconstrued by the time it gets out of my head and into the world. And love itself is still a fairly foreign concept to me if I'm being honest, something I'm working out in real time, mostly in these posts, mostly for her. So I suppose this is all part of the same project. Figuring out what I feel, what to do with it, and how to be useful without turning everything into a problem to solve.

With love, Daniel
