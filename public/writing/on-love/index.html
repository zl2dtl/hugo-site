On Love, Actually

March 10, 2026 · 5 min · 900 words · Daniel Liddle

This was supposed to be a tech blog. Somewhere along the line it became something else, and I think we both know whose fault that is. I write these because she makes me, and she somehow enjoys reading my reflections on her, written about her, largely for her, in what I can only describe as an awkward third person that we’ve both just agreed to pretend is normal. So here we are again.

I’ve been thinking about thinking about her, which is its own strange loop to be stuck in. And I’ve been thinking about love, what it actually is, what people mistake it for, and why the word itself feels completely inadequate for what I’m trying to describe.

Relationships are social constructs. That’s not a cynical thing to say, it’s just true. The labels we put on people, boyfriend, girlfriend, partner, just friends, it’s all language we invented to approximate something that doesn’t fit neatly into boxes. What I have doesn’t fit neatly into a box. There isn’t a word for it that doesn’t either undersell it or misrepresent it entirely, so I’ve stopped looking for one.

What I notice, looking around at the relationships people my age throw themselves into, is that a lot of what gets called love is really just the performance of love. It’s showy. It’s loud. It burns bright and fast and then it’s gone, three months later, and they’re onto the next one having apparently loved each other completely throughout. I don’t say that to be unkind. Puppy love is real in its own way. But it’s romanticised to the point where it collapses under its own weight the moment the world pushes back even slightly.

Because love is not showing off. Love is showing up. It’s being present and actually listening, not as a performance but as a default. It’s the quiet, unglamorous business of caring about someone consistently, not just when it’s easy or exciting or new.

I think about fireworks a lot when I think about this. A firework is genuinely beautiful. It lights up the whole sky, everyone looks, everyone gasps. And then it’s gone. Ten seconds, maybe. Nothing left but smoke.

A candle is a different thing entirely. There’s nothing showy about a candle. But there’s the gentle flicker of the orange flame, and the smell of the burning wick, and the small amount of warmth it throws into a room, and it lasts for years if you look after it properly. The thing is, you have to learn to appreciate a candle differently. You have to be close enough to notice it. You have to actually look.

And here’s the thing people miss. Both have power, just released differently. A candle can light a firework. The firework is brilliant and loud and everyone sees it, but it needed the candle to exist at all. And as long as the candle is never extinguished, it can light countless fireworks over a lifetime. The people who are only the firework get one brilliant moment and then they’re smoke.

But find someone who is also a candle, and something else happens entirely. Two candles, steady and constant, lighting each other’s fireworks. Every brilliant moment between them exists because the flame was already there, patient and quiet, waiting. And because neither of them burns out, there’s no limit to how many of those moments there can be. Just two people, a lifetime of fireworks, and the candle still going at the end of it.

People tell me I’m boring. That I’m missing out, that I should be out there making a mess of things with a rotating cast of people I’ll have forgotten by summer. And maybe to them it looks like I’m standing still. But I’ve just figured out how I want to spend my time, and more importantly, who I want to spend it with. That’s not boring. That’s just knowing what you have.

She is not mine in any possessive sense. But she is my person. There’s a difference, and it matters.

My person is the one I can’t wait to tell things to. Not the big things necessarily, just things. Something funny, something strange, something that happened in the corridor that was so outrageous it was almost impressive. She’s the first place my brain goes when something happens worth sharing, which is most things, because apparently everything is worth sharing when it’s her. She’s the one I’m still thinking about when I’m trying to shut my brain down for the night, which if you know me at all you’ll know is not a quick process under the best of circumstances. She makes it slower and I don’t mind even slightly.

She is my stability. Not in a way that puts any of that on her, that’s not fair to anyone, but in the sense that she is a constant I have built around quietly and without really deciding to. The world is loud and things fall apart and people disappoint you, and none of it feels quite as heavy when she’s there. What we have is stable in the way that actually useful things are stable. Not rigid, not loveless, not boring. Just grounded. Rational in the way that a decision you’ve thought through properly is rational. Controlled not because the feeling is small, but because we’re both sensible enough to hold it carefully.

With love, Daniel
